The Math Behind the Pizza

Why geometry matters when buying pizza

A bigger pizza always has more area. But more area at a higher price might still be a worse deal. Here's the math that settles it. 🍕

🔢 Try It: Enter Any Two Pizzas

The magic formula

A = πr²
Area = 3.14 × (radius × radius)
  • π (Pi) ≈ 3.14159… — the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter.
  • r (Radius) = Half the diameter. A 16" pizza has an 8" radius.
  • r² = Radius squared. For 8": 8 × 8 = 64
  • A = πr² = 3.14159 × 64 ≈ 201 sq in

The Pizzabyte System™

We measure pizza in Pizzabytes — each is 3 sq in (about one bite). This is the value metric.

Area alone doesn't tell you which pizza is the better deal — a huge pizza at a huge price can still be a rip-off. Cost per Pizzabyte factors in both size and price, so you're comparing apples to apples.

A 16" pizza = 67 Pizzabytes  |  A 12" pizza = 38 Pizzabytes

Lower cost per Pizzabyte = better value, full stop.

Big idea

A pizza's value isn't about size, and it's not even just about area. It's about cost per Pizzabyte — what each bite actually costs you after factoring in both size and price.

A complete 60-minute lesson. All tools referenced below are built into this page — click the inline links to jump directly to each one.
0–10 min

🎯 Hook: The Pizza Question

Ask: "If a 12" pizza costs $12 and a 16" pizza costs $16 — which is the better deal?" Take a show of hands. Most students say they're equal ($1/inch). Then reveal: you need to think about area, not diameter.

💡 Use the to show the visual immediately — students are always surprised how much bigger the large pizza is.
10–20 min

📖 Mini-Lesson: A = πr²

Walk through the formula using the Learn tab. Key steps:

  • Diameter vs. radius — always halve first
  • Square the radius (8 × 8 = 64)
  • Multiply by π ≈ 3.14159
  • 12" → 113 sq in, 16" → 201 sq in = 78% more pizza
💡 Common error: Students use diameter instead of radius. Have them write "÷ 2 first" on their worksheet.
20–35 min

📝 Data Collection & Calculation

Hand out the . Students research two real pizza sizes from a local restaurant and complete all five calculations by hand:

  • Radius (diameter ÷ 2)
  • Area (π × r²)
  • Cost per square inch (price ÷ area)
  • Pizzabytes™ (area ÷ 3)
  • Cost per Pizzabyte
💡 Differentiation: Calculators for on-grade. Advanced: compute by hand. Struggling: use Pizzabytes (friendlier whole numbers).
35–45 min

✅ Verification & Class Data Entry

🔢 Basic Statistics: What to Calculate

Once class data is complete, have students compute:

Mean cost/in²
Sum of all costs ÷ number of pizzas
What does a "normal" pizza cost in your area?
Range
Highest − Lowest cost/in²
Large range = big differences in value
Median
Middle value when sorted low to high
More robust than mean — ignores outliers
% where larger wins
Larger wins ÷ total × 100
Is the large always the better deal?

🔍 Discussion Questions

Q1

Which restaurant had the best deal overall? Is it a chain or local? Why might chains price differently?

Q2

Was the larger pizza always a better value? If not — why? Promotional smalls? Premium large pricing?

Q3

What was the class average cost/in²? A national average for cheese pizza is roughly $0.08–$0.12/sq in.

Q4

Look at your scatter plot. Does bigger diameter always mean lower cost/in²? Were there outliers?

Q5

If you had $20 to spend, which restaurant and size gives you the most pizza? Show your calculation.

Q6

The calculator only measures area. What factors did it miss? (Toppings, crust style, delivery fees, taste.)

🏆 Extension: The Class Best Deal Award

  • 🥇 Best Value — lowest cost per square inch
  • 💸 Worst Value — highest cost per square inch (maybe taste winner?)
  • 😲 Biggest Surprise — the result students least expected
  • 🏅 Most Consistent — smallest difference in cost/in² between sizes
💡 Assessment idea: Have students write a 3–5 sentence "Consumer Report" recommending the best deal for a family of 4 with a $25 budget, citing their data.

💡 Teaching Tips

  • Have students work in pairs — one computes, one checks. Mirrors real-world data work.
  • Watch for error #1: using diameter instead of radius (forgetting to divide by 2)
  • Watch for error #2: computing π × d² instead of π × r²
  • Pizzabytes™ is a great scaffolding tool — whole numbers are less intimidating
  • This lesson pairs well with unit pricing in grocery stores — same mathematical concept
  • For homework extension: students check two real deals at home and report back

What We Don't Calculate

Our calculator is awesome — but it's not magic

The bottom line

We calculate cost per square inch based on diameter and price. That's it! It's a great baseline, but real life has more variables. Use this as a guide, not the final word.

Crust thickness or topping coverage

We calculate area, but not crust-to-topping ratio. Deep dish vs. thin crust? Totally different!

Ingredient quality or oven type

Premium ingredients and wood-fired ovens make a difference. Sometimes the 'worse deal' is the better pizza!

Delivery fees and discounts

Fees, tips, and coupons can change the math. Always check the total cost!

The joy of leftover cold pizza

Some pizzas taste amazing cold. This is scientifically unmeasurable but highly important. 🤤
